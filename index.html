About

I am a Ph.D. researcher and Data Scientist with over ten years of experience designing, building, and deploying machine learning models for real-time analysis of high-dimensional, noisy data in production environments. My research sits at the intersection of ML systems and physical-layer monitoring, where I build efficient and scalable ML pipelines for communication infrastructure, cybersecurity, and intelligent fiber sensing.

My expertise spans the full ML pipeline ‚Äî from raw sensor data acquisition and feature engineering through model training, optimization, and real-time inference. I bring a strong foundation in information theory, statistical signal processing, optimization, and applied mathematics, enabling rigorous reasoning about system performance and efficiency bounds.

I am currently completing my Ph.D. in Electrical Engineering at Chalmers University of Technology (defence scheduled for April 2026) while working as a Research Assistant at Trinity College Dublin's CONNECT Centre. I have contributed to four major European research initiatives and built research partnerships spanning academia and industry across multiple countries.

Research Interests

ML for Optical Networks

Scalable machine learning pipelines for real-time anomaly detection and threat classification in production optical network infrastructure.

Fiber-Optic Sensing

Data-driven approaches for physical-layer fiber sensing using state of polarization analysis, with experimental validation on real-world deployments.

Physical-Layer Security

Detection and classification of eavesdropping, mechanical vibrations, and harmful events through analysis of optical signal characteristics.

Domain Adaptation

Autoencoder-based domain adaptation for cross-system generalization of ML classifiers across heterogeneous environments and hardware.

Signal Processing & Deep Learning

CNNs, autoencoders, and ensemble methods for streaming, high-dimensional time-series data under noisy real-world conditions.

Information Theory

Theoretical foundations including information-theoretic security bounds, secure degrees of freedom, and optimization for communication systems.
